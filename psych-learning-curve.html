Psych Learning Curve

Building APA's second-largest blog from the ground up—translating psychological science into practical resources for educators with minimal budget and maximum strategic impact

Role Creator, Editor, Content Strategist
Organization American Psychological Association
Timeline 2015–2020
Budget Under $1,000

The Challenge

APA's Education Directorate had valuable psychological research and expertise but no platform to reach the educators who needed it most. The directorate was historically under-resourced and overlooked by APA's central communications office. Teachers, students, and parents were searching for practical applications of psychology in educational settings, but APA wasn't positioned to serve that audience effectively.

The organizational hurdle: Before building anything, I had to convince APA's central communications office—who had largely ignored the Education Directorate—to approve a new publication initiative. They only became involved when they saw we were taking initiative, requiring a full strategic proposal and business case to secure buy-in from skeptical stakeholders who didn't naturally value our area.

Beyond organizational politics, there were significant resource constraints: minimal budget, no dedicated technical support, and limited staff capacity. The solution would need to be built from scratch on WordPress (migrating away from the enterprise Tridion CMS) with almost no financial resources.

The Strategic Vision: Building an Audience-to-Revenue Engine

Rather than creating just another organizational blog, I developed Psych Learning Curve as a multi-layered strategic asset designed to create a complete business cycle from audience development to revenue generation. This wasn't about building a popular blog—it was about creating a market intelligence platform that could identify and validate revenue opportunities through actual audience behavior.

  • 1
    Audience Development Through Trust

    Build a loyal, engaged audience by consistently publishing quality content on psychology applications in education—serving both "how to apply psych in teaching" and "how to teach psych."

  • 2
    Market Intelligence for Future Revenue

    Use engagement patterns and audience feedback to identify unmet needs and validate future product opportunities—learning what the audience would actually pay for rather than guessing.

  • 3
    Brand Authority Positioning

    Reinforce APA's position as the authoritative voice in education psychology by becoming the go-to resource where educators discover and apply psychological research.

  • 4
    Community Building Through Expert Voices

    Feature external field experts rather than only APA staff, positioning APA as the convener and platform while gaining better content, third-party credibility, and built-in distribution through contributor networks.

  • 5
    Network Amplification

    Leverage social media to spotlight featured experts, creating organic reach as contributors shared their features with their own audiences—effectively turning each article into a distribution channel.

The Build

With approval secured and strategy defined, I faced the technical reality: building a professional publication platform from scratch with virtually no budget.

Technical Implementation

  • Learned WordPress from the ground up to build a custom site (APA's enterprise Tridion CMS wasn't suitable for this kind of regular publishing)
  • Designed and implemented custom theme that aligned with APA brand standards while creating a distinct identity for education content
  • Built content architecture supporting multiple audience segments (teachers, students, parents) with clear navigation and discovery paths
  • Implemented SEO best practices to ensure content could be discovered by educators searching for practical psychology applications
  • Integrated social sharing functionality to facilitate organic distribution
  • Created sustainable publishing workflows for regular content production with limited staff

Content Development

  • Established editorial standards and voice guidelines that made complex psychological research accessible without oversimplification
  • Developed relationships with field experts willing to contribute original content and participate in interviews
  • Created recurring content formats: expert interviews, news roundups, practical teaching guides, student perspectives
  • Built email newsletter strategy to drive consistent readership and engagement
  • Positioned myself as translator and curator—making psychological science practical for educators

Content Approach: Writing That Connects

The content needed to serve multiple audiences while maintaining credibility with both educators and psychologists. I developed an approach focused on practical application, expert voices, and accessible language.

Featured Article: "Psychology and Trauma in Schools"
Expert Interview Format • April 2018

This piece demonstrates the blog's core approach: identifying urgent educator needs (understanding trauma-informed practices), connecting with authoritative experts (Drs. Santiago and Raviv), and translating complex research into actionable guidance.

"Teachers know first-hand how trauma can make learning nearly impossible for a student and they've seen how it has become increasingly more common among school-aged American students. As school shootings and gun violence have captured the attention of many, there's a more significant problem lurking in the classroom. It's the student chronically exposed to trauma, and it's often masked by stigma and shame."

The article structures complex multi-tiered support systems into digestible Q&A format, includes visual frameworks, provides actionable next steps, and positions APA as the trusted resource connecting educators with psychological expertise.

Each piece was designed to:

  • Start with the educator's actual problem or question
  • Bring in expert voices for credibility and depth
  • Translate research into practical application
  • Provide concrete next steps or resources
  • Create shareable moments (pull quotes, key insights)

Proof of Concept: Top 20 Principles Case Study

One piece of content perfectly demonstrates how the strategic vision worked in practice—translating committee-produced research into market intelligence that led directly to revenue.

The Translation Challenge

An APA committee had produced "Top 20 Principles from Psychology for PreK-12 Teaching and Learning"—a 72-page research document with extensive methodology, citations, and academic framing. Authoritative, comprehensive, and practically inaccessible for K-12 teachers.

Content Strategy

I recognized the opportunity to translate this academic research into a practical blog post that K-12 teachers would actually read and use. The post distilled the principles, made them actionable, and presented them in classroom-friendly language.

The blog's hero image captured the essence of the translation work: making psychological principles clear and accessible for K-12 educators. This visual approach helped position complex research as immediately actionable classroom guidance.

Performance

The Top 20 Principles post quickly became the highest-performing piece on the entire blog, driving the largest percentage of traffic for an extended period. But more importantly, the sustained engagement wasn't just a vanity metric—it was a clear demand signal.

Strategic Response

Rather than celebrating traffic and moving on, we recognized what the data was telling us: strong, sustained interest in Top 20 Principles indicated genuine market demand. This led directly to developing a $75 certification program for schools wanting to demonstrate adherence to the principles—a badges program that generated actual revenue.

What This Demonstrates: The complete validation of the strategic vision. Audience building generated market intelligence (traffic patterns showing demand) that led to a revenue product (certification program) based on proven interest rather than assumptions. The blog wasn't just content—it was the market research engine that identified what people would actually pay for.

Note: Top 20 Principles wasn't the strategy itself—it was evidence the strategy worked. The blog's multi-layered approach created conditions where strong content could reveal market opportunities worth pursuing.

What Made It Work

Strategic Positioning

Rather than trying to compete in the crowded "education blog" space, I identified a specific niche: the intersection of psychological research and educational practice. This positioning gave us a defendable market position and clear value proposition.

Community Over Audience

By featuring external experts and positioning APA as convener rather than sole voice, we built a community of contributors who had investment in the platform's success. Each featured expert became an advocate and distributor.

Consistency as Strategy

Regular publishing cadence built trust and habit. Readers knew when to expect content and what quality to expect. This reliability turned casual visitors into loyal subscribers.

Content That Serves

Every piece started with "what does the educator need?" rather than "what does APA want to say?" This user-first approach ensured content actually solved problems rather than promoting organizational priorities.

Smart Resource Constraints

Limited budget forced creative solutions—leveraging contributor networks for content and distribution, using social media for reach, building simple but effective WordPress infrastructure. Constraints drove innovation rather than limiting it.

Lessons Learned

Engaged beats large: A smaller, highly engaged audience is more valuable than massive traffic with low engagement. Our 15%+ email engagement rate (vs. industry standard 2-3%) proved people genuinely valued what we created.

Contributors as partners: Featuring external experts wasn't just about content quality—it created a network of advocates who promoted the platform because their reputations were tied to it.

Constraints drive creativity: Having almost no budget forced strategic thinking about what actually mattered. We couldn't buy our way to success, so we had to build something genuinely valuable.

Platform as intelligence: An engaged audience tells you exactly what they need through their behavior, questions, and feedback. The Top 20 Principles example perfectly demonstrates this—traffic patterns revealed demand that justified building a certification program. The blog was a continuous market research engine that identified revenue opportunities through actual behavior.

Organizational change through results: Proving value through measurable outcomes shifted how central communications viewed the Education Directorate. Success creates internal credibility that proposals and arguments can't.
